Most people who end up in strategic communications started there. They studied politics or journalism, joined an agency, and worked their way through media lists and stakeholder maps. My path was different. I started in data science, spent years inside enterprise technology projects, and arrived at government relations with a conviction that the industry was sitting on a mountain of underutilised information.

My first professional chapter was at PwC Netherlands, where I worked as a technology consultant implementing Oracle ERP Cloud solutions across supply chain and global trade management. The work was deeply technical — configuring systems, mapping data flows, and helping large organisations make sense of the information locked inside their operations. What struck me was not the technology itself but the gap between the data organisations collected and the decisions they actually made with it. The most sophisticated ERP implementation in the world is worthless if the insights it produces never reach the people who need them.

That observation pulled me toward Hill & Knowlton Strategies, where I joined the ESG and Sustainability Advisory team as a Senior Account Executive. The remit was fascinating: helping companies monitor, manage, and report on ESG performance at a time when regulatory frameworks like the CSRD were transforming sustainability from a communications exercise into a data discipline. I saw first-hand how communications professionals struggled with the volume and complexity of the data they were expected to interpret. At the same time, I was building prompt engineering workflows and Python tools internally — early experiments in using AI to bridge the gap between raw information and strategic narrative.

The move to Duke Strategies sharpened the focus further. Working within the data domain at a firm specialising in corporate affairs and communications, I concentrated on converting data into actionable intelligence for clients navigating complex stakeholder environments. It became clear that the future of government relations and crisis management belonged to firms that could combine deep domain expertise with genuine technical capability — not technology bolted on as an afterthought, but data science embedded in the strategic process from the start. That conviction is what led to Stromy, where we are building the intelligence infrastructure that makes this possible at scale.
